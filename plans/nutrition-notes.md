## Approach

Principles-based, not rigid meal plans. You decide what to eat. The system gives you targets, guardrails, and the reasoning behind them. Track protein — the single most important variable. Everything else is guidelines.

## The Science Behind the Targets

### Deficit Size: Why ~400 kcal/day

**Garthe et al. (2011):** Compared 0.7% BW/week loss (slow) vs 1.4% BW/week (fast) in athletes. Both groups lost ~4 kg total. The slow group **gained** 1 kg of lean mass while losing fat. The fast group lost 0.3 kg of lean mass. The slow group also showed greater strength improvements.

**Helms et al. (2014):** Recommended 0.5-1% BW/week loss rate for natural athletes during a cut. At the higher end (1%), lean mass losses increase, especially as body fat drops below ~15%.

**At 88.6 kg, targeting 0.5-0.7% BW/week = 0.44-0.62 kg/week.** This requires a deficit of approximately 350-500 kcal/day (since ~7,700 kcal ≈ 1 kg of fat tissue). Starting at ~400 kcal/day hits the middle of this range.

**Why not more aggressive?** You're also restarting a training program and adding running. Stacking a large deficit on top of new training stimulus is the #1 way to crash adherence by week 3-4. A moderate deficit you can sustain for 12+ weeks outperforms an aggressive deficit you abandon after 4.

### Protein: Why ≥170 g/day

**The evidence hierarchy for protein during a cut:**

| Source | Recommendation | Context |
|--------|---------------|---------|
| Morton et al. (2018) meta-analysis | 1.6 g/kg/day sufficient for hypertrophy | Energy balance, not deficit |
| Helms et al. (2014) systematic review | 2.3-3.1 g/kg FFM | Resistance-trained, caloric restriction |
| 2024 meta-analysis (energy-restricted, trained) | Up to 1.9 g/kg BW or 2.5 g/kg FFM | Less FFM loss with higher intake |
| Garthe et al. (2011) | High protein preserved LBM during slow loss | Athletes, 1.6-1.8 g/kg |

**Your numbers:**
- Body weight: 88.6 kg
- Fat-free mass: ~69.8 kg
- 1.6 g/kg BW = 142 g/day (minimum)
- 2.0 g/kg BW = 177 g/day (target)
- 2.2 g/kg BW = 195 g/day (upper range)
- 2.5 g/kg FFM = 175 g/day (Helms' FFM-based target)

**Convergence point: ~170-180 g/day.** This is the target. Below 150 g/day is a problem. Above 200 g/day is fine but unnecessary — the extra protein displaces carbs and fats that support training performance and hormonal health.

### Protein Distribution: Why 4 Meals

**Leucine threshold:** Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) requires ~2.5-3 g of leucine per meal to be maximally stimulated (Moore et al., 2009; Schoenfeld & Aragon, 2018). This translates to ~30-40 g of high-quality protein per meal for an ~89 kg male.

**Meal frequency:** MPS is refractory — after a protein-rich meal, MPS is elevated for ~3-5 hours, then returns to baseline even if amino acids are still available. This means spreading protein across 3-4 meals spaced 3-5 hours apart optimizes total daily MPS vs eating the same amount in 1-2 meals.

**Practical:** At 170 g/day ÷ 4 meals = ~42 g/meal. At 3 meals = ~57 g/meal (harder to consume, less optimal MPS distribution but still effective).

**Intermittent fasting:** Some evidence suggests IF is compatible with muscle retention during a cut (Moro et al., 2016), but the shortened eating window makes hitting 170 g protein harder and reduces MPS stimulation frequency. Not recommended during this cut unless you strongly prefer it.

## Core Targets

| Parameter | Target | Range | Non-negotiable? |
|-----------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| **Calories** | ~2,500 kcal/day | 2,300-2,600 | Adjustable based on rate of loss |
| **Protein** | ≥170 g/day | 160-195 g | **Yes — this is the floor** |
| **Fat** | ≥65 g/day | 65-85 g | Minimum for hormonal health |
| **Carbs** | Remainder (~230-280 g) | Flexible | Fill after protein and fat targets are met |
| **Fiber** | ≥25 g/day | 25-35 g | Satiety + gut health |
| **Water** | ≥2.5 L/day | 2.5-3.5 L | More on training/running days |
| **Meals** | 3-4/day | — | Space protein across meals |

### How the Macros Break Down

| Macro | Grams | Calories | % of 2,500 kcal |
|-------|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Protein | 170 g | 680 kcal | 27% |
| Fat | 75 g | 675 kcal | 27% |
| Carbs | 285 g | 1,140 kcal | 46% |
| **Total** | — | **2,495 kcal** | **100%** |

### Why This Fat/Carb Split

**Minimum fat:** Hormonal health requires a minimum fat intake. Below ~0.5 g/kg BW (~44 g for you), testosterone and other steroid hormones can be suppressed (Helms et al., 2014; Hämäläinen et al., 1984). The target of ~75 g (0.85 g/kg) provides comfortable headroom above the minimum.

**Carb priority after protein and fat:** Carbohydrates fuel glycolytic exercise (heavy lifting, intense efforts). During a deficit, carbs are the first thing to get squeezed — but they directly affect training performance. Keeping carbs at ~3.2 g/kg supports 4 lifting sessions/week and 2 runs. If you go below ~200 g/day consistently, expect gym performance to suffer.

## TDEE Estimation

| Component | Value | Method |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| BMR (scale) | ~1,878 kcal | Bioimpedance |
| BMR (Mifflin-St Jeor) | ~1,865 kcal | 10 × 88.6 + 6.25 × 182 − 5 × 33 − 5 |
| BMR (average) | ~1,870 kcal | Consensus of both methods |
| Activity multiplier | 1.55 | Moderate: 4 gym + 2 runs + daily walking |
| TDEE estimate | ~2,900 kcal | BMR × multiplier |
| Deficit | ~400 kcal | Moderate (14% below maintenance) |
| **Target intake** | **~2,500 kcal** | |

**Critical caveat:** TDEE is an *estimate*. Metabolic rate varies by ±10-15% between individuals with the same stats. The only way to know your actual TDEE is to observe your weight response to a known caloric intake over 2-3 weeks.

### Calibration Protocol (Weeks 1-3)

1. Eat ~2,500 kcal/day (or just track protein and eat "normally")
2. Weigh yourself every morning (fasted, after bathroom, before food/water)
3. Use the **weekly average**, not daily weight (daily weight fluctuates 1-2 kg from water, food, sodium)
4. After 2-3 weeks, assess:
   - Losing 0.4-0.6 kg/week average? → Perfect. Stay here
   - Losing <0.3 kg/week? → Reduce intake by 100-200 kcal (cut carbs or fat, not protein)
   - Losing >0.8 kg/week? → Increase intake by 100-200 kcal. Too aggressive
   - Weight unchanged? → Reduce by 200-300 kcal. You're at maintenance, not in a deficit

## Tracking Method

### Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): Protein-Only Tracking

Track one number: grams of protein per day. That's it.

**Why start here:**
- Simplest possible system that captures the most important variable
- Reduces overwhelm when you're also learning a new gym program
- Most people who track "everything" burn out on tracking before they burn fat
- If you're eating ~170 g protein and limiting alcohol and junk food, calories will naturally land in a reasonable range

**How to estimate protein without a food scale:**
| Food | Approximate protein |
|------|-------------------|
| Palm-sized portion of meat/fish (~150 g) | 30-40 g |
| 2 whole eggs | 12 g |
| 1 cup Greek yogurt | 15-20 g |
| 1 scoop whey protein | 24-27 g |
| 1 cup cottage cheese | 25-28 g |
| 1 cup lentils/beans (cooked) | 15-18 g |
| 100 g tofu | 10-12 g |
| 1 cup milk | 8 g |
| 30 g cheese | 7 g |

### Phase 2 (Weeks 5+ if needed): Full Macro Tracking

Switch to tracking all macros if:
- Weight loss has stalled for 2+ weeks despite consistent protein
- You suspect you're eating significantly more than 2,500 kcal
- You want more precision and it doesn't stress you out

Use an app (MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, MacroFactor). Log everything for 1-2 weeks to calibrate, then return to estimation if the habit holds.

## Meal Timing

### What Matters (Evidence: Moderate-Strong)

| Guideline | Why | Source |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Protein at every meal | Each meal triggers independent MPS response | Moore et al. (2009) |
| 30-50 g protein per meal | Leucine threshold for MPS at your body weight | Schoenfeld & Aragon (2018) |
| Don't skip protein post-workout | Anabolic window is real but wide (~4-6 hours, not 30 min) | Aragon & Schoenfeld (2013) |
| Pre-sleep protein | Casein or protein-rich snack before bed extends overnight MPS | Res et al. (2012); Snijders et al. (2015) |

### What Doesn't Matter Much (Evidence: Weak)

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Eat within 30 min post-workout" | The anabolic window is 4-6 hours. If you ate a protein-rich meal 2-3 hours before training, you're fine. Don't stress |
| "Carbs after 6pm make you fat" | Total daily intake matters, not timing. Eat carbs whenever they fit your schedule |
| "Fasted cardio burns more fat" | Burns more fat *during* the session, but total 24-hour fat oxidation is the same (Schoenfeld et al., 2014) |

### Practical Meal Template (Example)

| Meal | Timing | Protein Target | Example |
|------|--------|---------------|---------|
| Breakfast | Morning | 35-45 g | 3 eggs + toast + Greek yogurt |
| Lunch | Midday | 40-50 g | Chicken/fish + rice + vegetables |
| Pre/post workout | Around training | 30-40 g | Protein shake + banana (or part of another meal) |
| Dinner | Evening | 40-50 g | Meat/fish + potatoes/pasta + salad |
| Optional snack | Before bed | 40 g | Cottage cheese, casein shake, or yogurt. 40 g > 20 g for overnight MPS (Trommelen 2018/2023) |

This is a *template*, not a prescription. Eat what you like. Hit protein.

## Carb Timing Around Training

**This is a minor lever — not worth stressing about.** But if you want to optimize:

| Timing | Recommendation | Why |
|--------|---------------|-----|
| 1-3 hours pre-training | Meal with carbs (40-60 g) + protein (30 g) | Glycogen availability for heavy sets |
| Post-training | Meal with carbs + protein within 2-3 hours | Glycogen replenishment, MPS |
| Running days | Normal eating | Zone 2 runs don't deplete glycogen meaningfully at 5 km |

**If training fasted in the morning:** have at least a protein shake beforehand (25-30 g whey). Training completely fasted in a deficit increases muscle protein breakdown risk.

## Diet Breaks and Refeeds

### Evidence: MATADOR Study (Byrne et al., 2018)

Intermittent energy restriction (2 weeks deficit / 2 weeks maintenance) vs continuous deficit:
- Intermittent group lost significantly more fat
- Less metabolic adaptation (smaller resting metabolic rate reduction)
- 6 months post-diet: intermittent group maintained 8 kg more weight loss

### Application to This Program

| Week | Calories | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| 1-4 | ~2,500 (deficit) | Fat loss |
| **5 (deload week)** | **~2,900 (maintenance)** | **Diet break — eat at maintenance for 1 week. Align with gym deload** |
| 6-8 | ~2,500 (deficit) | Fat loss |
| **9 (deload week)** | **~2,900 (maintenance)** | **Diet break — reassess everything** |

**During diet break weeks:**
- Increase calories to maintenance (~2,900) primarily from carbs (+100-150 g carbs)
- Keep protein the same (≥170 g)
- Keep fat roughly the same
- This is NOT a "cheat week." It's strategic. Eat at maintenance, not above
- Benefits: leptin normalization, thyroid hormone recovery, psychological relief, glycogen replenishment, better training performance during deload week

## Alcohol

**Impact during a cut:**

| Effect | Magnitude | Source |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Calories | 7 kcal/g (beer: ~150 kcal, wine: ~120 kcal, spirits: ~100 kcal per serving) | — |
| MPS suppression | Alcohol doses ≥0.5 g/kg BW (~3-4 drinks) suppress MPS by 20-30% for 12-24 hours | Parr et al. (2014) |
| Fat oxidation | Alcohol halts fat oxidation while being metabolized (liver prioritizes alcohol clearance) | Siler et al. (1999) |
| Sleep quality | Disrupts REM sleep even at moderate doses (1-2 drinks) | Ebrahim et al. (2013) |
| Recovery | Impairs glycogen resynthesis, increases inflammation | Barnes (2014) |

**Recommendation:** You're already reducing alcohol. During this cut:
- 0-2 drinks/week is ideal
- If drinking, prefer spirits + zero-cal mixer (lowest calorie option)
- Never drink the night before a heavy training session (squat/deadlift day)
- Count alcohol calories toward your daily intake
- One social night per week with moderate drinking will not derail the cut — but more than that accumulates

## Supplements

### Tier 1: Strong Evidence

| Supplement | Dose | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| **Creatine monohydrate** | 5 g/day | 2024 meta-analysis (12 RCTs): +1.14 kg lean mass, -0.7 kg fat vs placebo. During energy restriction, creatine group lost 1.4% FFM vs 2.4% for placebo | Take daily, timing doesn't matter. Expect 1-2 kg water weight gain in week 1 — this is intracellular water, not fat. **Continue throughout the cut** |
| **Whey protein** | As needed to hit target | Convenient protein source. 25-30 g per scoop | Not magic. Just food in powder form. Use if struggling to hit 170 g from whole foods |
| **Caffeine** | 1-3 mg/kg before training (~100-250 mg) | Ergogenic: improves strength, endurance, and perceived effort. Mild appetite suppressant | Don't take after 2 PM if it affects sleep. Tolerance develops — cycle off occasionally |

### Tier 2: Moderate Evidence / Conditional

| Supplement | Dose | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| Vitamin D | 1,000-2,000 IU/day | Deficiency is common and impairs muscle function, mood, immunity | Get blood levels tested if possible. More important in winter |
| Omega-3 (fish oil) | 1-2 g EPA+DHA/day | Anti-inflammatory, may support recovery. Modest evidence for body composition | Not a priority if eating fatty fish 2x/week |
| Magnesium | 200-400 mg/day (glycinate or citrate) | Supports sleep quality, muscle relaxation, recovery | Take before bed if using for sleep |

### Not Recommended

| Supplement | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| Fat burners / thermogenics | Minimal effect, side effects, not worth the money |
| BCAAs | Redundant if eating adequate protein. Literally a subset of whey protein |
| Testosterone boosters | No OTC product meaningfully raises testosterone in healthy males |
| CLA | Meta-analyses show trivial fat loss (~0.1 kg/week). Not worth it |

## Hunger Management

During a deficit, hunger is normal. Persistent, unbearable hunger is a sign the deficit is too aggressive or food choices are poor.

### Evidence-Based Satiety Strategies

| Strategy | Why It Works | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **High protein** | Protein is the most satiating macronutrient (Paddon-Jones et al., 2008) | Already built into the plan at ≥170 g/day |
| **High fiber** | Slows digestion, increases meal volume | Vegetables, legumes, whole grains, fruits |
| **High volume / low calorie density** | Stomach stretch receptors signal fullness | Salads, soups, vegetables, fruits, popcorn |
| **Adequate hydration** | Dehydration mimics hunger signals | 2.5-3.5 L/day |
| **Minimize liquid calories** | Liquids don't trigger satiety as effectively as solid food | Water, coffee, tea > juice, soda, smoothies |
| **Sleep ≥7 hours** | Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin (hunger hormone) by 15-25% (Spiegel et al., 2004) | Prioritize sleep as a fat loss tool |

### Foods with High Satiety Index (per calorie)

Boiled potatoes, eggs, oatmeal, apples, oranges, fish, lean meat, beans, vegetables (especially cruciferous), Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, popcorn (air-popped).

### Foods to Be Aware Of (low satiety per calorie)

Nuts and nut butters (very calorie-dense despite being healthy), oils, cheese, dried fruit, granola, pastries, chips.
These aren't "bad" — just easy to overeat in a deficit. Portion awareness matters more with these foods.

## Red Flags

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|--------------|--------|
| Hunger ≥4 consistently for >1 week | Deficit may be too aggressive | Increase by 100-200 kcal (from carbs). Check protein is on target |
| Energy ≤2 consistently for >1 week | Recovery compromised | Check sleep first. Then increase calories by 100-200 kcal |
| Binge-restrict pattern forming | Psychological unsustainability | Loosen tracking, increase calories to maintenance for 1 week, reassess approach |
| Losing >0.8 kg/week for 2+ weeks | Too aggressive — likely losing muscle | Increase calories by 200 kcal |
| Losing <0.2 kg/week for 3+ weeks | Not in a meaningful deficit | Reduce by 100-200 kcal or audit tracking accuracy |
| Obsessive food thoughts / anxiety | Tracking causing harm | Switch to protein-only or qualitative tracking. The mental health cost outweighs the precision benefit |
| Performance declining despite adequate sleep | Deficit + training load too high | Diet break at maintenance for 1 week |

## Metabolic Adaptation: What It Is and How to Handle It

**What happens:** During a prolonged deficit, your body reduces energy expenditure through:
1. **Reduced BMR** — metabolic rate drops beyond what weight loss alone would predict (~5-15% additional reduction)
2. **Reduced NEAT** — you subconsciously move less, fidget less, take fewer steps
3. **Reduced TEF** — eating less food = less energy spent digesting food
4. **Hormonal changes** — leptin decreases (more hunger), thyroid hormones decrease (lower metabolic rate), cortisol increases

**How to mitigate:**
1. **Diet breaks every 4-6 weeks** (already programmed in weeks 5 and 9)
2. **Maintain step count** (8,000-10,000/day — counteracts NEAT reduction)
3. **Keep protein high** (preserves lean mass, which preserves BMR)
4. **Resistance training** (the strongest signal to preserve metabolically active muscle tissue)
5. **Adequate sleep** (≥7 hours — sleep deprivation amplifies all of the above)
6. **Don't reduce calories too aggressively** — small, measured reductions (100-200 kcal) when needed

**Reframe:** Metabolic adaptation is real but not a brick wall. It slows progress, it doesn't stop it. The solution is patience + strategic breaks, not more extreme restriction.
