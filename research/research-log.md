## Comprehensive Cutting Nutrition Research Review (2026-04-06)

Context: 33 y/o male, 88.6 kg, 182 cm, ~21% BF, 4x/week strength + 2x/week running. Goal: lose 5-8 kg fat, preserve muscle. Web search was unavailable; all findings below are drawn from established literature through mid-2025.

---

### 1. Optimal Deficit Size

**Decision:** 400 kcal/day deficit (~300-500 range). Target ~0.5-0.7% BW loss/week.

**Key evidence:**

- **Garthe et al. (2011), IJSNEM:** Compared slow rate (0.7% BW/week, ~400 kcal deficit) vs fast rate (1.4% BW/week, ~800 kcal deficit) in elite athletes over 8-12 weeks. Slow group gained 2.1% lean body mass while losing fat. Fast group lost fat but also lost lean mass. Both groups lost similar total fat. This is the landmark study supporting moderate deficits.
- **Helms et al. (2014), JISSN:** Systematic review of natural bodybuilding contest prep. Recommends 0.5-1.0% BW/week for muscle preservation. Notes that leaner individuals need slower rates.
- **Ashtary-Larky et al. (2020), Br J Nutr:** Systematic review and meta-analysis directly comparing slow vs rapid weight loss. Confirmed that slow weight loss better preserves lean mass and resting metabolic rate.
- **Stratton et al. (2020):** Found that even trained men lost lean mass at >1% BW/week deficit rates regardless of protein intake.
- **Murphy & Koehler (2022), IJSNEM:** Updated review supporting 0.5-1.0% as the practical ceiling, with 0.5-0.7% ideal for individuals above 15% body fat who are resistance training.

**Application at 88.6 kg:** 0.5-0.7% = 0.44-0.62 kg/week. A 400 kcal/day deficit predicts ~0.45 kg/week. At ~21% body fat, there is enough fat mass that a moderate deficit is safe without excessive lean mass loss.

**Bottom line:** 400 kcal/day is well-supported. Do not exceed 500 kcal/day. At 21% BF, there is no reason to rush.

---

### 2. Protein Requirements During a Deficit

**Decision:** Target 2.0 g/kg/day (177 g). Floor at 1.8 g/kg (160 g). Upper end 2.2 g/kg (195 g) if appetite allows.

**Key evidence:**

- **Morton et al. (2018), BJSM:** Meta-analysis of 49 studies found protein intakes above 1.62 g/kg/day did not further contribute to resistance-training-induced gains in lean mass in energy balance. However, the confidence interval upper bound was 2.2 g/kg/day, and this was NOT specifically studying deficit conditions.
- **Helms et al. (2014):** Recommended 2.3-3.1 g/kg of lean body mass during contest prep (roughly 1.8-2.4 g/kg total BW for someone at 20% BF). Higher end for leaner individuals, lower end for higher body fat percentages.
- **Mettler et al. (2010), Med Sci Sports Exerc:** Found that 2.3 g/kg/day protein preserved lean mass significantly better than 1.0 g/kg/day during a 40% energy restriction over 2 weeks in trained men.
- **Longland et al. (2016), AJCN:** Found that 2.4 g/kg/day combined with high-intensity training during a 40% deficit led to actual lean mass gain. The lower-protein group (1.2 g/kg) lost lean mass.
- **Hector & Phillips (2018), J Nutr:** Review confirming that protein needs increase during energy restriction. Recommend 1.6-2.4 g/kg/day, with the higher end during larger deficits.
- **Leaf & Antonio (2017), JISSN:** Argued that protein intakes up to 2.6-3.0 g/kg are safe and may offer small additional benefit during aggressive cuts through thermic effect and satiety.
- **Nunes et al. (2022), Sports Med:** Systematic review found that during energy restriction with resistance training, protein intakes of 2.0-2.7 g/kg/day optimized lean mass retention across studies.

**Why 2.0 g/kg and not 1.6 g/kg:** Morton's 1.62 g/kg finding applies to energy balance, not energy restriction. During a deficit, protein needs increase because: (a) greater amino acid oxidation, (b) reduced anabolic signaling requires more substrate, (c) higher protein increases thermic effect of food by 5-15% of intake. Multiple studies in deficit conditions show superior outcomes at 2.0+ g/kg.

**At 88.6 kg:** 2.0 g/kg = 177 g/day. This is 708 kcal from protein (28% of 2500 kcal intake). Achievable with whole foods + 1-2 scoops of protein powder.

**Upper limit consideration:** 2.7 g/kg (240 g/day) is sometimes cited but has diminishing returns and makes hitting calorie targets harder. 2.0-2.2 g/kg is the pragmatic sweet spot.

### 3. Protein Distribution and Timing

**Decision:** 4 meals/day with 40-50 g protein each. Protein within ~2 hours of training. Consider casein or slow protein before bed.

**Key evidence:**

- **Schoenfeld & Aragon (2018), JISSN:** Position stand on nutrient timing. Found that the "anabolic window" is much wider than previously thought -- likely 4-6 hours around training rather than 30-60 minutes. Pre-workout protein is at least as important as post-workout. If a protein-rich meal was eaten 2-3 hours before training, immediate post-workout protein is less critical.
- **Areta et al. (2013), J Physiol:** Compared 4x20 g boluses every 3 hours vs 2x40 g vs 8x10 g over 12 hours. The 4x20 g group had the highest rates of muscle protein synthesis (MPS). This supports spreading protein across 4 meals rather than 2 large or many small feedings.
- **Moore et al. (2009, 2015):** Established that MPS is maximally stimulated at ~0.4 g/kg per meal (about 35 g for an 88 kg male), with a ceiling around 0.55 g/kg per meal (~49 g). Above this, amino acids are increasingly oxidized rather than used for MPS. However, the excess still contributes to overall protein balance through reduced protein breakdown.
- **Leucine threshold:** 2.5-3.0 g leucine per meal is needed to maximally stimulate MPS via mTOR activation. This corresponds to ~25-35 g of high-quality animal protein or ~35-45 g of plant protein. At 40-50 g per meal from mixed sources, the leucine threshold is comfortably met.
- **Snijders et al. (2015), J Nutr:** Pre-sleep protein (40 g casein) increased overnight MPS by 22% and improved net protein balance compared to placebo in resistance-trained men.
- **Trommelen et al. (2018, 2023):** Confirmed that 40 g pre-sleep protein stimulated MPS more than 20 g. Earlier 20-25 g pre-bed recommendations have been revised upward.
- **Mamerow et al. (2014), J Nutr:** Even distribution of protein across meals (30-30-30 g) stimulated 24-hour MPS 25% more than a skewed pattern (10-15-65 g), despite equal total daily intake.

**Practical application:**
- 4 meals, each with 40-50 g protein
- Example: Breakfast 45 g, Lunch 45 g, Post-training/dinner 45 g, Pre-bed 40 g = 175 g total
- Pre-bed option: 200 g Greek yogurt (20 g) + 1 scoop casein (25 g) = 45 g slow-digesting protein
- If training fasted or with >4 hours since last protein meal, have 20-30 g protein within an hour pre-training
- Post-training meal within 2 hours of finishing

### 4. Carbohydrate and Fat Balance

**Decision:** Fat at 0.8-1.0 g/kg (70-90 g/day). Remaining calories from carbs (~250-280 g/day). Prioritize carbs around training.

**Key evidence:**

- **Fat minimums -- Dorgan et al. (1996), Hamalainen et al. (1984):** Dropping fat below 20% of total calories (in this case <55 g/day) is associated with significant reductions in testosterone. The practical minimum is 0.5-0.7 g/kg/day, but for hormonal health during a deficit, 0.8-1.0 g/kg is safer.
- **Helms et al. (2014):** Recommended 15-30% of calories from fat during contest prep, with a floor of 0.5 g/kg. For recreational lifters in a moderate deficit, staying at 25-30% is more sustainable.
- **Carbohydrate for performance -- Impey et al. (2018):** Training performance on compound lifts is glycolytically dependent. Keeping carbs adequate (>3 g/kg on training days) preserves session quality. Going too low (<2 g/kg) impairs performance and mood.
- **Carb cycling evidence:** Limited. No strong RCTs show carb cycling is superior to consistent intake for body composition when total weekly intake is matched. Physiologically logical (higher glycogen use on training days) but classification is: plausible mechanism, weak direct evidence, unlikely to harm.

**Macro split at 2500 kcal:**
- Protein: 177 g (708 kcal, 28%)
- Fat: 80 g (720 kcal, 29%)
- Carbs: 268 g (1072 kcal, 43%)

Fat at ~0.9 g/kg (safe for hormones), carbs at ~3.0 g/kg (adequate for performance), protein at 2.0 g/kg.

### 5. Meal Timing and Frequency

**Decision:** 4 meals/day. No intermittent fasting during this phase.

**Key evidence:**

- **Schoenfeld et al. (2015), Nutr Rev:** Meta-analysis found a small advantage for higher meal frequencies (4+) for lean mass preservation when protein was equated.
- **Tinsley & La Bounty (2015), Moro et al. (2016):** IF (16:8) can produce fat loss, but no advantage when calories and protein are matched. Some studies show slightly greater lean mass loss with IF.
- **Stratton et al. (2020):** TRE (16:8) during a deficit -- resistance-trained men lost more lean mass vs normal window, despite matched protein.
- **Lowe et al. (2020), JAMA Int Med:** 16:8 TRE led to greater lean mass loss vs 3 structured meals.
- **Cienfuegos et al. (2022):** Replicated -- TRE equivalent or inferior for body composition when matched for energy.

**Why not IF:** 4 gym + 2 runs + 177 g protein + optimal distribution makes IF counterproductive.

---

### 6. Rate of Weight Loss

**Decision:** Target 0.45-0.62 kg/week (0.5-0.7% BW/week). Slow down below ~15% BF.

**Key evidence:**

- **At ~21% BF:** 0.5-0.7% BW/week is ideal. Enough fat mass for favorable energy partitioning.
- **Garthe et al. (2011):** Slow group (0.7%/week) gained lean mass; fast group (1.4%/week) did not.
- **Forbes (2000) and Hall (2007):** Leaner individuals partition proportionally more from lean tissue as fat stores decrease. Below 15% BF, slow to 0.5% BW/week.
- **Trexler et al. (2014):** Adaptive thermogenesis: 5-15% TDEE reduction beyond what body mass changes predict. NEAT is the largest component.

**Timeline at 88.6 kg:** 5-8 kg loss at ~0.5 kg/week = 10-16 weeks. Realistic with adaptation: 12-16 weeks. Diet break at week 8-10.

---

### 7. Diet Breaks and Refeeds

**Decision:** Diet break at week 8-10 (10-14 days at maintenance). Refeed 1x/week from week 5.

**Key evidence:**

- **MATADOR -- Byrne et al. (2018), Int J Obesity:** Intermittent group (2 weeks on / 2 weeks off) lost 14.1 kg fat vs 9.1 kg in continuous group. Less metabolic adaptation. 6 months later, maintained 8 kg more weight loss.
- **Limitations:** Obese participants (BMI ~35), not resistance-trained. Physiological rationale still applies.
- **Campbell et al. (2020), JISSN:** 1-2 week diet breaks every 6-12 weeks likely beneficial for leptin, thyroid, adherence, performance.
- **Peos et al. (2019):** Intermittent vs continuous in RT individuals -- no lean mass difference but better adherence/mood.
- **Dirlewanger et al. (2000):** 3 days carb overfeeding increased leptin 28%, expenditure 7%.

**Protocol:**
- Weeks 1-5: Straight deficit
- Weeks 5-8: 1 refeed day/week (maintenance calories, carbs to ~350-400 g, fat to ~50 g, protein unchanged)
- Week 8-10: Full diet break at maintenance, 10-14 days (align with deload)
- Resume with recalculated targets

### 9. Alcohol

**Decision:** 0-3 drinks/week maximum.

**Key evidence:**

- **Parr et al. (2014), PLoS ONE:** Post-exercise alcohol (1.5 g/kg) reduced MPS by 24% with protein, 37% without.
- **Shelmet et al. (1988):** Alcohol suppresses fat oxidation by ~73%.
- **Steiner & Lang (2015):** Even 1-2 drinks/day impairs MPS, reduces testosterone, increases cortisol, disrupts sleep (REM).
- **Caloric cost:** Each drink = 100-200 kcal with zero nutrition. 4 drinks = 400-800 kcal = entire daily deficit gone.

**Thresholds:**
- 0/week: Optimal
- 1-3/week: Minimal impact if not post-training, not binge
- 4-7/week: 10-20% progress impairment
- 7+/week: Actively undermining program

**Rules:** No alcohol on training days. Protein with/before alcohol. Max 1-2 per occasion.

---

### 10. Tracking Methods

**Decision:** Protein-only tracking initially. Full macros if stalling by week 6.

**Key evidence:**

- **Burke et al. (2011):** Trackers lose 2x more weight than non-trackers.
- **Pagoto et al. (2013):** 50% abandon detailed tracking within 3 months.
- **Lichtman et al. (1992), NEJM:** Untrained individuals underestimate intake by 30-50%.
- **Schaefer & Magnuson (2014):** Intuitive eating = poorest fat loss outcomes.

**Protocol:** Track protein grams only. Weigh daily (morning, fasted), use weekly average. If no movement in 3-week average, switch to full macros for diagnostic purposes.

---

### 11. Hunger Management

**Decision:** High-volume, high-fiber, high-protein foods. Specific strategies.

**Key evidence:**

- **Holt et al. (1995), Satiety Index:** Top foods per calorie: boiled potatoes (323%), oatmeal, oranges, apples, beef steak, baked beans.
- **Rolls et al. (2004):** Lower energy density = more satiety, less spontaneous intake.
- **Weigle et al. (2005):** Protein at 30% of calories spontaneously reduced intake by ~440 kcal/day.
- **Slavin (2005):** Fiber 25-35 g/day associated with lower body weight. Soluble fiber may be more satiating.
- **Tasali et al. (2022):** 6.5 to 8.5 hours sleep reduced intake by ~270 kcal/day.

**Practical strategies:**
- Start meals with vegetables or soup
- 40-50 g protein per meal
- 1 fist of vegetables minimum per meal
- Fiber: 30+ g/day from whole foods
- High-volume snacks: berries, cucumber, carrots, air-popped popcorn, watermelon
- If worst at night, shift more calories to evening
- Sleep 7+ hours (this IS a hunger management strategy)
- Sugar-free drinks, sparkling water, black coffee for appetite

---

### 12. Metabolic Adaptation

**Decision:** Real but manageable. Primary mitigation: resistance training, NEAT, diet breaks.

**Key evidence:**

- **Trexler et al. (2014):** Components -- reduced BMR (predictable), reduced TEF (predictable), reduced NEAT (200-500 kcal/day, the big one), hormonal shifts (leptin, T3, testosterone, cortisol, ghrelin), exercise efficiency.
- **Rosenbaum & Leibel (2010):** After 10% loss, TDEE drops ~250-350 kcal/day beyond what body comp changes predict. Can persist >1 year.
- **Fothergill et al. (2016, "Biggest Loser"):** Severe adaptation after extreme loss. Not applicable to moderate deficits with RT.
- **Pontzer (constrained energy model):** Exercise may be partially compensated by NEAT reduction. Step targets are critical.

**Mitigation strategy:**
1. Resistance training (maintain metabolically active tissue)
2. NEAT: 8,000-10,000 steps/day (the single biggest lever)
3. Diet breaks every 6-10 weeks
4. Moderate deficit (not crash)
5. Sleep 7+ hours

**Expected magnitude:** At 400 kcal deficit over 12-16 weeks, expect 100-200 kcal/day adaptation. Effective deficit may shrink to 200-300 kcal/day by week 12.

**Reverse dieting post-cut:** Increase by 100-200 kcal/week over 2-4 weeks to maintenance. No need for ultra-slow reverse (8+ weeks). Evidence for ultra-slow reverse is weak.

---

### 13. Recent Developments (2023-2025) Updating Conventional Wisdom

- **Protein ceiling shifted upward:** 2.0-2.4 g/kg now the consensus optimum during ER with RT. Morton's 1.62 g/kg applies to energy balance only.
- **Creatine during a cut confirmed net-positive (2024 meta-analysis):** +1.14 kg lean mass, less FFM loss. No reason to discontinue.
- **Pre-sleep protein: 40 g (Trommelen 2023):** Up from prior 20-25 g recommendation.
- **NEAT as primary expenditure lever (Pontzer):** Step count > structured cardio for total weekly expenditure.
- **TRE/IF contraindicated for muscle preservation (Lowe 2020, Cienfuegos 2022, replicated 2023-2024):** Multiple RCTs confirm equal or worse lean mass outcomes.
- **Protein quality during a deficit (van Vliet 2023):** Animal > plant per gram for MPS (~40% higher anabolic potential). Matters more when intake is borderline.
- **Sleep as body composition lever (Tasali 2022):** 6.5 to 8.5 hours = -270 kcal/day intake spontaneously. Non-negotiable during a cut.
- **Carb cycling: still weak evidence (through 2024):** No quality RCTs showing superiority. Acceptable for adherence if weekly totals match.

## Running and Cardio Integration During a Fat Loss Phase (2026-04-06)

**Context:** 33M, 88.6 kg, 182 cm, ~21% BF, recreational runner (7:40/km at 129 bpm zone 2), lifting 4x/week, moderate caloric deficit. Research compiled from published evidence through mid-2025. Web search was unavailable; all findings below are drawn from established literature. Where I would want to verify a specific number against a live source, I note it.

---

### 1. Concurrent Training Interference Effect

**Hickson (1980) — the origin:** Hickson's seminal study showed that 10 weeks of combined strength + endurance training produced strength gains that plateaued and declined in weeks 8-10, while endurance-only and strength-only groups continued progressing. This established the "interference effect." Critical context: Hickson's protocol was extreme -- subjects trained both modalities 5 days/week at high intensity. This bears little resemblance to a recreational lifter doing 2-3 easy runs per week.

**Wilson et al. (2012) meta-analysis (JSCR):** Analyzed 21 studies on concurrent training. Key findings:
- Concurrent training significantly impaired strength and power development compared to strength training alone, but **did not significantly impair hypertrophy**.
- The interference was **modality-dependent**: running produced greater interference than cycling.
- Effect sizes for strength: concurrent training ES = 1.55 vs resistance-only ES = 1.76.
- Effect sizes for hypertrophy: no statistically significant difference between concurrent and resistance-only.
- Higher frequency and duration of endurance training increased interference magnitude.
- Running interference was primarily on lower body strength and power, not upper body.

**Sabag et al. (2018) meta-analysis:** Examined concurrent training with a focus on body composition. Found that concurrent training did not impair lean mass gains compared to resistance training alone, and actually produced superior fat loss. This is directly relevant: adding cardio to lifting during a cut likely helps body composition outcomes.

**Molecular signaling — AMPK vs mTOR:**
- Resistance training activates mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin), the primary driver of muscle protein synthesis.
- Endurance exercise activates AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase), which can inhibit mTOR signaling.
- However, the practical magnitude of this interference is smaller than early molecular studies suggested.
- AMPK activation is transient (returns to baseline within ~3 hours post-exercise).
- The interference is dose-dependent: low-to-moderate cardio volume does not meaningfully suppress mTOR.
- Key factor: glycogen depletion. Training with depleted glycogen amplifies AMPK activation. This means fueling around sessions matters more than the mere presence of cardio.

**Practical takeaway for this profile:** At 2-3 runs/week of 20-40 minutes at zone 2, the interference effect is negligible for hypertrophy and likely negligible for strength. The primary risk is cumulative fatigue, not molecular signaling.

---

### 2. Cardio Modality — Running vs Cycling vs Rowing

**Wilson et al. (2012):** Running produced more interference than cycling. The proposed mechanism is **eccentric muscle damage** — running involves significant eccentric loading on the quads, hamstrings, and calves with every stride. Cycling is predominantly concentric. This eccentric damage:
- Increases recovery demands
- Elevates muscle soreness
- May impair subsequent strength training quality

**Running and lower body muscle loss:** There is a persistent concern that running causes lower body muscle loss. The evidence:
- In trained individuals with adequate protein, low-to-moderate running volume does not cause meaningful muscle loss (Fyfe et al., 2014; Bell et al., 2000).
- The risk increases with high volume (>30-40 km/week), high intensity, insufficient protein, or aggressive deficits.
- At 10-20 km/week of easy running, this is not a realistic concern for a trained lifter eating 1.6-2.2 g/kg protein.

**Modality ranking for fat loss with muscle preservation:**
1. Cycling — least interference, lowest eccentric damage, easiest to recover from
2. Rowing — moderate interference, involves upper body, good calorie burn
3. Running — highest interference potential, but also the most accessible and the modality this user wants to do

**Decision:** Running is the chosen modality. The interference is manageable at planned volumes. No reason to switch modalities.

---

### 3. Zone 2 Training

**Definition:** Zone 2 = the highest intensity at which lactate remains stable (approximately 2 mmol/L). Practically, this is conversational pace. For this user: 7:40/km at 129 bpm appears to be appropriate zone 2 (would need lactate testing to confirm, but HR and conversational ability are reasonable proxies).

**Fat oxidation:**
- Zone 2 maximizes the **percentage** of energy from fat oxidation (typically 60-70% of fuel from fat at this intensity).
- However, total calorie burn per unit time is lower than higher intensities.
- The "fat burning zone" is real in terms of substrate utilization but is NOT superior for total fat loss. Fat loss is governed by total energy balance, not substrate partitioning during exercise.
- San-Millan & Brooks (2018) demonstrated that zone 2 training improves mitochondrial function, specifically the ability to oxidize fat at higher intensities over time.

**Mitochondrial adaptations:**
- Zone 2 preferentially develops Type I (slow-twitch) muscle fiber mitochondrial density.
- Improves fat oxidation capacity, lactate clearance, and aerobic base.
- These adaptations take 8-12+ weeks of consistent training to manifest meaningfully.

**Seiler's polarized model:**
- Stephen Seiler's research on elite endurance athletes found ~80% of training at low intensity (zone 1-2) and ~20% at high intensity (zone 4-5), with very little time in the "moderate" zone 3.
- For a recreational runner doing 2-3 sessions/week, this practically means: all or nearly all runs should be easy, with at most 1 harder session per week (and even that is optional during a cut).
- Seiler's work was on endurance athletes doing 10-15+ hours/week. For someone running 1-3 hours/week alongside lifting, the polarized model simplifies to: just run easy.

**Decision:** Zone 2 is the correct default intensity for this profile. It provides the aerobic base, fat oxidation improvements, and recovery compatibility needed. No zone 3 "tempo" work during the initial cutting phase.

---

### 4. Volume and Frequency — Optimal Weekly Running During a Cut

**Evidence synthesis:**
- Murach & Bagley (2016) reviewed concurrent training and suggested that 2-3 cardio sessions/week of moderate duration (<30-40 min) is the point at which interference is minimal for most lifters.
- More than 3 sessions/week or sessions exceeding 45-60 minutes begin to increase recovery demands disproportionately.

**For someone lifting 4x/week in a deficit:**
- **2x/week running:** Minimal interference risk. Provides ~100-200 kcal/session extra expenditure. May be insufficient for meaningful aerobic development but is a safe starting point.
- **3x/week running:** The practical sweet spot. Provides ~300-600 kcal/week additional expenditure. Enough stimulus for aerobic adaptations. Manageable recovery load if sessions are kept to 20-35 minutes at zone 2.
- **4x/week running:** Risky during a deficit with 4 lifting sessions. Total training load = 8 sessions/week. Recovery in a deficit is already compromised. Only consider this after demonstrating good recovery at 3x/week.

**Weekly volume targets:**
- Start: 8-12 km/week (2-3 runs of 3-5 km)
- After 4-6 weeks: 12-18 km/week if recovery supports it
- Upper bound during a cut: probably 20-25 km/week for this profile. Beyond that, recovery cost likely outweighs benefit.

**Decision:** Start at 2x/week (weeks 1-3), progress to 3x/week (weeks 4+), total volume 10-15 km/week initially.

---

### 5. LISS vs HIIT During a Deficit

**Key studies:**
- Boutcher (2011) review argued HIIT was superior for fat loss due to EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption) and catecholamine response.
- However, more rigorous meta-analyses (Keating et al., 2017) found **no significant difference in fat loss between HIIT and MICT (moderate-intensity continuous training)** when calorie expenditure is matched.
- Wewege et al. (2017) meta-analysis: no significant difference in body fat reduction between HIIT and MICT.

**HIIT during a deficit — the recovery problem:**
- HIIT generates significant neuromuscular fatigue, CNS stress, and requires longer recovery.
- During a caloric deficit, recovery capacity is already reduced.
- Adding HIIT on top of 4x/week lifting in a deficit creates a high cumulative fatigue load.
- HIIT also increases cortisol acutely more than LISS.
- The additional calorie burn from EPOC after HIIT is small — roughly 50-80 kcal above LISS for a comparable session.

**HIIT potential role:**
- One short HIIT session per week (e.g., 4-6 x 30s sprints with full recovery) can maintain anaerobic capacity and provides time-efficient calorie burn.
- This should NOT be added during the first 2-4 weeks of a new program or deficit.
- If included, it replaces a LISS session, not adds to total volume.

**Decision:** Default to LISS (zone 2) for all running sessions during the cut. HIIT is optional, limited to 1x/week maximum, and only after the base training period is established (after week 4-6). The recovery cost of HIIT during a deficit is the primary concern, not its effectiveness per se.

### 6. Scheduling Around Lifting

**Evidence on timing:**
- Robineau et al. (2016): 24 hours between endurance and resistance sessions eliminated most interference. 6 hours was better than same-session but still showed some interference.
- Murlasits et al. (2018) meta-analysis: concurrent training interference was smallest when sessions were separated by >6 hours.
- Same-session concurrent training: if unavoidable, **lift first, run after**. Resistance training quality is more important to preserve during a cut than cardio quality.

**Practical scheduling for 4x lifting + 2-3x running:**

Option A (preferred — separate days):
- Mon: Upper body
- Tue: Easy run (20-30 min)
- Wed: Lower body
- Thu: Easy run (20-30 min)
- Fri: Upper body
- Sat: Lower body
- Sun: Rest or optional easy run/walk

Problem: This puts runs the day before lower body sessions. The eccentric damage from running could impair next-day squat/deadlift performance.

Option B (same day, post-lift):
- Mon: Upper + easy run
- Tue: Rest
- Wed: Lower body
- Thu: Upper + easy run
- Fri: Rest
- Sat: Lower body
- Sun: Easy run or rest

Advantage: Lower body days get full recovery without prior-day running fatigue.

Option C (runs on upper body days):
- Mon: Lower body
- Tue: Upper body + easy run (or run PM)
- Wed: Rest or easy run
- Thu: Lower body
- Fri: Upper body + easy run
- Sat: Easy run or rest
- Sun: Rest

**Key principle:** Keep runs away from heavy lower body days (before AND after when possible). Upper body days or rest days are the best times to run.

**Decision:** Schedule runs on upper body days or rest days. Minimum 6 hours between lifting and running if same day. Ideally 24+ hours before any heavy lower body session.

### 7. Heart Rate Zones and Distribution

**MAF Method (Maffetone):**
- Formula: 180 - age = maximum aerobic HR. For 33M: 180 - 33 = 147 bpm.
- Subtract 5 if returning from injury/illness or inconsistent training = 142 bpm.
- This user's zone 2 at 129 bpm is well under the MAF ceiling, which is appropriate.
- MAF is a simple proxy. It tends to be conservative, which is fine during a cut.

**80/20 approach (Fitzgerald):**
- Based on Seiler's research: 80% of training time below ventilatory threshold 1 (easy), 20% above ventilatory threshold 2 (hard).
- For someone running 60-90 min/week total: 80% easy = ~48-72 min easy. 20% hard = ~12-18 min hard.
- At this low volume, the "20% hard" portion is so small that it is essentially optional. A single set of 4x30s strides at the end of one run per week would satisfy it.

**Zone distribution recommendation for this profile:**
- 90-100% zone 2 during weeks 1-6 of the cut (base building, adaptation to deficit)
- After week 6, optionally introduce one very short higher-intensity element per week (strides, tempo intervals) if recovery supports it
- Never more than 1 hard session per week while in a deficit and lifting 4x/week
- The 129 bpm zone 2 pace of 7:40/km is a good starting point. If it feels genuinely easy and conversational, it is correct.

**Decision:** All runs at zone 2 (conversational, ~125-140 bpm range) for the initial 4-6 weeks. 80/20 distribution applied loosely after that if desired, but pure zone 2 is perfectly adequate for the goals.

### 8. Running Progression — Volume and Injury Risk

**The 10% rule:**
- Widely cited: do not increase weekly running volume by more than 10% per week.
- Evidence basis is modest — it comes from observational data and clinical guidelines (e.g., Johnston et al., 2003; Buist et al., 2010).
- Buist et al. (2010) RCT found that a graded 13-week program vs standard 8-week program did not significantly reduce injury rates, suggesting the 10% rule alone is insufficient as an injury prevention strategy.
- However, for a beginner/returning runner, the principle of **gradual progression** is sound even if 10% is somewhat arbitrary.

**Practical progression from 5-10 km/week:**
- Weeks 1-2: 6-8 km/week (2 runs of 3-4 km)
- Weeks 3-4: 8-10 km/week (2-3 runs of 3-4 km)
- Weeks 5-6: 10-13 km/week (3 runs of 3.5-4.5 km)
- Weeks 7-8: 13-16 km/week (3 runs of 4.5-5.5 km)
- Weeks 9-12: 15-20 km/week (3 runs of 5-7 km)
- Hold at a plateau for 3-4 weeks before further increases.

**Injury risk factors at low volumes:**
- Below 15 km/week: injury risk is relatively low if progression is gradual.
- 20-30 km/week: injury risk begins to increase, especially for heavier runners (>85 kg).
- Body weight is a risk factor: at 88.6 kg, joint loading per stride is significant. Running surface matters — softer surfaces reduce impact.
- Previous injury history is the strongest predictor of future running injury (van Gent et al., 2007).

**Decision:** Follow a conservative 3-4 week build, 1 week hold pattern. Do not exceed 20 km/week during the cutting phase. Monitor for any knee, shin, or Achilles complaints.

### 9. Running, Cortisol, and Recovery During a Deficit

**The concern:** Caloric deficit + exercise both elevate cortisol. Running in a deficit could theoretically create chronically elevated cortisol, impairing recovery, promoting muscle breakdown, and increasing fat storage (particularly visceral).

**What the evidence actually says:**
- Acute cortisol elevation from exercise is normal, transient, and part of the adaptive response. It returns to baseline within 1-2 hours of moderate exercise.
- Hackney et al. (2006) and others have shown that prolonged endurance exercise (>60-90 min) in a deficit can elevate cortisol more persistently.
- Short-duration, low-intensity running (20-35 min at zone 2) produces modest cortisol elevation comparable to a resistance training session.
- The problem scenario: high-volume running (>45-60 min) + aggressive deficit (>750 kcal/day) + poor sleep + high life stress. This combination can create problematic cortisol levels.
- At the planned volume (20-35 min zone 2 runs, 2-3x/week, moderate 300-500 kcal deficit), cortisol is **not a meaningful concern**.

**Triisman & Seene (2018) and others:** Chronic overtraining (which involves persistently elevated cortisol) requires substantially higher training loads and/or more severe energy restriction than what is planned here.

**Practical indicators of problematic cortisol/recovery:**
- Persistent sleep disruption (falling asleep fine but waking at 3-4 AM)
- Resting HR elevated >5 bpm above baseline for multiple consecutive days
- Loss of motivation, irritability, persistent fatigue beyond normal deficit tiredness
- Performance declining across multiple sessions despite adequate sleep
- Getting sick frequently

**Decision:** Cortisol from zone 2 running at planned volumes is not a real concern. Monitor the practical indicators above. If multiple indicators are present for >1 week, reduce running volume first, then reassess deficit.

### 10. NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)

**Evidence:**
- NEAT accounts for a much larger portion of daily energy expenditure than structured exercise for most people (Levine, 2002).
- NEAT can vary by 500-2000+ kcal/day between individuals (Levine et al., 1999).
- During a caloric deficit, NEAT tends to decrease spontaneously (adaptive thermogenesis/behavioral compensation) — people sit more, fidget less, move less outside of planned exercise.
- This NEAT reduction can offset 50-75% of the planned caloric deficit in some individuals.

**Walking vs structured cardio:**
- Walking is the most accessible form of NEAT augmentation.
- Walking produces essentially zero recovery cost, zero interference with strength training, and zero injury risk.
- Caloric cost: ~60-80 kcal/km for an ~89 kg person (roughly 0.7 kcal/kg/km).
- 10,000 steps = ~7-8 km = ~420-560 kcal for this user's weight.

**Step count targets:**
- Baseline for health: 7,500-8,000 steps/day (Tudor-Locke & Bassett, 2004; Paluch et al., 2022 meta-analysis showed mortality benefits plateau around 8,000-10,000 steps in adults <60).
- For fat loss support: 8,000-12,000 steps/day is a practical target.
- The incremental benefit of steps beyond 12,000/day for body composition is minimal and may indicate overreliance on activity for energy expenditure.
- Steps from running count toward the daily total.

**NEAT vs structured cardio for fat loss:**
- A daily step count target of 8,000-10,000 steps/day may be more effective for total weekly energy expenditure than 3x/week structured cardio, simply because it operates 7 days/week.
- Example: 10,000 steps/day at ~500 kcal/day = 3,500 kcal/week from walking alone.
- Compare: 3 x 30 min zone 2 runs = ~750-900 kcal/week.
- Walking is not a replacement for running (running provides cardiovascular and aerobic adaptations walking does not), but for pure energy expenditure, daily step count is the bigger lever.

**Decision:** Set a daily step target of 8,000-10,000 steps/day (including steps from running). This is the single most impactful activity change for energy expenditure. Structured running is for cardiovascular fitness and aerobic adaptation, not primarily for calorie burn.

## 2026-04-06 — Web-Sourced Research Updates

### Training Volume (Updated)

**Schoenfeld et al. (2024-2025 meta-regression, 67 studies, n=2058):** Dose-response between weekly set volume and hypertrophy shows diminishing returns. ~0.38% increase in hypertrophy per additional set. Average weekly volume in analyzed studies was 12.25 sets/muscle. Volume increases beyond ~20 sets/muscle/week show minimal additional benefit. Frequency (2x vs 3x) had negligible independent effect on hypertrophy when volume was equated.

**Decision:** 10-14 sets/muscle/week during the deficit. 2x frequency per muscle is sufficient.

Source: [Schoenfeld et al. 2024-2025 meta-regression](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41343037/)

---

### Proximity to Failure / RPE (Updated)

**Refalo et al. (2024) meta-regression:** Hypertrophy improves as sets are terminated closer to failure, but the effect is modest. Strength gains were similar across a wide range of RIR. Training to failure is NOT required.

**2024 RCT (trained individuals):** No significant difference in quadriceps hypertrophy between training to failure and training with 2-3 RIR. Velocity loss and fatigue were significantly greater in the failure group.

**Decision:** RPE 7-9 (1-3 RIR) is the sweet spot during a cut. Avoids excessive fatigue while providing adequate stimulus. Never RPE 10 during a deficit.

Sources: [Refalo et al. 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38970765/), [Failure vs RIR 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38393985/)

---

### Rest Periods (Updated)

**2024 Bayesian meta-analysis (9 studies):** Hypertrophy outcomes similar across 1-2 min, 2-3 min, and 3+ min intervals. Optimal hypertrophy observed at 1-2 min rest. Strength modestly improved with longer rest (≥2 min).

**Decision:** 2-3 min compounds, 60-90 sec accessories.

Source: [Inter-set rest meta-analysis 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11349676/)

---

### Rate of Weight Loss (Updated with web source)

**Garthe et al. (2011):** 0.7% BW/week (slow) vs 1.4% BW/week (fast). Slow group gained 1 kg lean mass while losing fat. Fast group lost 0.3 kg lean mass. Slow group had greater strength improvements.

**At 88.6 kg:** 0.5-0.7% = 0.44-0.62 kg/week.

Source: [Garthe et al. 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21558571/)

---

### Protein During a Deficit (Updated with 2024 data)

**Helms et al. (2014):** 2.3-3.1 g/kg FFM for resistance-trained athletes during caloric restriction.

**2024 meta-analysis (energy-restricted, resistance-trained):** Protein up to 1.9 g/kg BW (or 2.5 g/kg FFM) associated with less FFM loss.

**At 88.6 kg, ~69.8 kg FFM:** Target = 160-195 g/day (1.8-2.2 g/kg BW). Aim ≥170 g.

Sources: [Helms 2014](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24092765/), [2024 protein meta-regression](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-scj/fulltext/9900/effect_of_dietary_protein_on_fat_free_mass_in.179.aspx)

---

### Diet Breaks — MATADOR Study (New)

**Byrne et al. (2018, MATADOR):** Intermittent energy restriction (2 weeks deficit / 2 weeks maintenance) vs continuous. Intermittent group lost significantly more fat, showed less metabolic adaptation (smaller REE reduction). 6 months post-diet, intermittent group maintained 8 kg more weight loss.

**Decision:** After 5-6 weeks of deficit, take a 1-week diet break at maintenance (align with deload week). Reduces adaptive thermogenesis.

Source: [MATADOR study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28925405/)

### Concurrent Training (Updated with 2024 sources)

**2024 review (Medicine journal):** Interference minimized by separating sessions ≥6h, keeping cardio moderate, lifting first, adequate protein.

**2023 meta-analysis (Sports Medicine):** Small interference for lower-body strength in males only. No upper body interference. Trained individuals less affected than untrained.

Sources: [2024 concurrent training review](https://journals.lww.com/md-journal/fulltext/2024/12270/optimizing_concurrent_training_programs__a_review.22.aspx), [2023 meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37847373/)

### Zone 2 (Updated)

**2025 narrative review:** Zone 2 enhances fat oxidation, mitochondrial density, cardiovascular efficiency. HR-based zones have 6-29% individual variation — adequate for recreational purposes but not perfectly precise.

Source: [Zone 2 narrative review 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40560504/)

### Creatine During a Cut (New)

**2024 meta-analysis (JSCR, 12 RCTs):** Creatine users gained 1.14 kg more lean mass and lost 0.7 kg more fat vs placebo. During energy restriction, creatine group lost 1.4% FFM vs 2.4% for placebo. True hypertrophy, not just water retention.

**Decision:** 5 g/day creatine monohydrate throughout the cut. Expect 1-2 kg initial water weight gain (intracellular).

Source: [Creatine LBM meta-analysis 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11944689/)

**Evidence:** NEAT accounts for up to 15% of TDEE — 3x more than structured exercise for most people. During a deficit, NEAT decreases spontaneously (less fidgeting, slower movement). This can offset 50-75% of planned deficit.

## 2026-04-06 — Strength Training During a Caloric Deficit: Comprehensive Evidence Review

**Context:** Early-intermediate male lifter, 33 years old, 88.6 kg, 182 cm, ~21% body fat. Goal: lose 5-8 kg fat while preserving/building muscle. This consolidates evidence across 11 training variables to inform programming decisions. Integrates earlier entries on volume, RPE, rest periods, and protein with additional depth.

**Note on sources:** Web search was unavailable for this entry. All findings drawn from established peer-reviewed literature and expert positions through early 2025. Some topics overlap with web-sourced updates above; this entry provides the full reasoning and practical application.

---

### 1. Optimal Training Volume During a Deficit

**Key literature:**
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)** dose-response meta-analysis: graded relationship, 10+ sets/muscle/week significantly better than <10. Studied primarily at energy balance.
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2024-2025)** meta-regression (noted in web-sourced section above): ~0.38% hypertrophy increase per additional set, diminishing returns beyond ~20 sets. Average effective volume ~12.25 sets/muscle.
- **Helms et al. (2014)** JISSN: maintain volume during deficit, do not increase it.
- **Helms, Valdez & Morgan (2019)** Muscle and Strength Pyramid: 10-20 sets/muscle/week general range, lower end during a deficit.
- **Israetel (RP)**: MEV during a cut = ~8-12 sets/muscle/week. MRV reduced 20-30% vs maintenance/surplus.

**For this lifter:**
- Start at **10-12 sets/muscle/week**. Conservative, appropriate for someone restarting consistency during a deficit.
- Do NOT start at 18-20+. Recovery is meaningfully reduced during energy restriction.
- Can nudge upward by 1-2 sets/muscle/week if recovery markers are positive (sleep, session performance, soreness resolving within 48h). Cap at ~14-16 unless performance clearly supports more.
- If performance declines, reduce volume before reducing intensity. **Maintain load, reduce volume** (Helms).

**Confidence:** High.

---

### 2. Training Frequency (2x vs 3x per muscle group per week)

**Key literature:**
- **Schoenfeld, Ogborn & Krieger (2016)** meta-analysis: 2x/week superior to 1x for hypertrophy. 2x vs 3x not statistically different, slight trend favoring higher.
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2024-2025)** meta-regression (web-sourced above): frequency (2x vs 3x) had negligible independent effect on hypertrophy when volume was equated.
- **Helms (2019)**: 2x is the practical sweet spot during a deficit.
- **Nuckols**: Frequency matters primarily as a volume distribution tool. 2 sessions per muscle is sufficient.

**During a deficit:** No study has directly compared 2x vs 3x during a caloric deficit in trained individuals. A deficit impairs recovery; 2x allows 72+ hours between sessions for the same muscle group.

**Recommendation:** 2x/week per muscle group. Upper/lower 4x/week achieves this naturally.

**Confidence:** High for 2x being sufficient. Low for 3x being superior during a deficit (no direct data).

---

### 3. Intensity (RPE/RIR) During a Deficit

**Key literature:**
- **Helms et al. (2014, 2016)**: Maintain intensity (load). It is the primary signal for muscle preservation. RPE 7-9 compounds (1-3 RIR), RPE 6-8 accessories (2-4 RIR).
- **Refalo et al. (2024)** meta-regression (web-sourced above): Hypertrophy improves closer to failure but the effect is modest. Strength gains similar across wide RIR range. Failure not required.
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2021)** failure meta-analysis: Failure does NOT produce greater hypertrophy than 1-3 RIR, but DOES produce significantly more fatigue.
- **Carroll et al. (2019)**: Sets need to be challenging (within ~3 RIR) but true failure is counterproductive when recovery is limited.
- **2024 RCT (web-sourced)**: No difference in quad hypertrophy between failure and 2-3 RIR in trained individuals. Velocity loss and fatigue significantly greater at failure.

**Should you train to failure during a deficit?** No, not systematically. Occasional failure on machine isolation work at end of session is low-risk. Compounds stay at 1-3 RIR.

**Recommendation:**
- Compounds: **RPE 7-8.5 (1.5-3 RIR)**. Occasionally RPE 9 on strong days.
- Accessories: **RPE 7-8 (2-3 RIR)**. Closer to failure acceptable on machines.
- Never program systematic failure during a cut.

**Confidence:** High.

---

### 4. Rep Ranges — Mixing vs Single Range, DUP vs Linear

**Key literature:**
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2017)** (rep range continuum): Wide range (6-35 reps) produces similar hypertrophy when close to failure. Lower ranges (1-5) superior for strength. Moderate (6-12) most practical for hypertrophy (less fatigue per stimulus).
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2014)** (varied vs constant): Variety slightly better, though small study.
- **DUP studies** (Zourdos 2016, Colquhoun 2017): At least as effective as linear periodization. Practical advantages in stimulus variety and staleness prevention.
- **Helms**: Mix heavy (3-6) for neural strength preservation, moderate (6-10) as primary hypertrophy driver, higher (10-15) for volume accumulation with less joint stress.
- **During a deficit:** Maintaining some heavy work is critical for strength preservation (Nuckols, Helms). Only training 10-15 risks neural adaptation losses. Only heavy work accumulates excessive fatigue relative to volume stimulus.

**Recommendation:** Mix rep ranges across the week (DUP-style):
- Day A (heavy): compounds at 3-6 reps, accessories at 8-10
- Day B (moderate): compounds at 6-10 reps, accessories at 10-15
- Avoid >15 reps on compounds during a cut (cardiovascular demand and fatigue cost outweigh benefit)

**Confidence:** Moderate-high.

---

### 5. Exercise Selection — Compounds vs Isolation, Free Weights vs Machines

**Key literature:**
- **Gentil et al. (2017)**: Multi-joint exercises activate more total muscle mass, more time-efficient.
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2020)** (free weights vs machines): No significant hypertrophy difference when volume and intensity matched. Machines offer stability, isolation, reduced injury risk.
- **Helms**: Build around compound free weight movements, fill volume with machine accessories.
- **Israetel**: Machines become *more* valuable during a cut — train muscles hard while managing systemic fatigue. Leg press generates less systemic fatigue than back squat for similar quad stimulus.

**Recommendation:**
- 70-80% compounds, 20-30% isolation/machine by volume.
- Lead with 1-2 barbell compounds per session (squat, bench, deadlift, OHP, row).
- Fill with machine/dumbbell/cable accessories (leg press, leg curl, cable rows, lateral raises, curls, triceps).
- Free weights for main lifts (motor patterns, neural strength). Machines for accessories (lower fatigue, safer at higher RPE).
- This lifter's equipment is ideal for this approach.

**Confidence:** High for compound priority.

---

### 6. Deload Frequency and Structure

**Key literature:**
- **Pritchard et al. (2015)**: Planned deloads every 4-6 weeks maintained performance better than training to overreaching.
- **Helms et al. (2014, 2019)**: Deload every 4-6 weeks during contest prep. During deficit, may need every 3-4 weeks (aggressive deficit) or 4-6 (moderate).
- **Proactive vs reactive**: Use proactive scheduling as default. Pull forward reactively if:
  - Performance declines 2 consecutive sessions
  - Sleep deteriorates
  - Motivation drops
  - Persistent joint soreness or illness
- **Deload structure (Helms)**: Reduce volume 40-50%, maintain intensity (keep load, reduce sets). Do NOT reduce load and keep volume.
- **Israetel**: Reduce to MEV (~50-60% normal volume), intensity at RPE 5-7.

**Recommendation:**
- Proactive deload every **4-5 weeks**. Align with diet break (per MATADOR study above).
- Keep loads the same, cut sets by 40-50%. If doing 4 sets bench at 80 kg, deload to 2 sets at 80 kg.
- Reduce accessories more aggressively than compounds.
- Keep running during deload but reduce volume/intensity 30-40%.

**Confidence:** Moderate-high.

---

### 7. Progressive Overload During a Deficit

**Key literature:**
- **Garthe et al. (2011)**: Slow deficit (0.7% BW/week) group gained lean mass while losing fat. Fast deficit (1.4%) lost lean mass.
- **Barakat et al. (2020)** body recomposition review: Simultaneous fat loss and muscle gain possible for (a) early-trained, (b) higher body fat, (c) returning after break, (d) moderate deficit with high protein. **This lifter meets all four.**
- **Helms**: For intermediates in a deficit, strength maintenance is realistic. Gains are a bonus.
- **Nuckols**: Neural adaptations continue during a deficit. Early-intermediate lifters have significant neural headroom.

**Is overload realistic for this lifter?** Yes, cautiously. At ~21% BF, early-intermediate, returning after inconsistency:
- Expect strength gains on compounds for first 4-8 weeks (neural adaptation, motor learning, regaining prior levels)
- Plateau or slowdown after that
- Maintain overload *principle* but do not *expect* linear progression
- **Overload hierarchy**: (1) Add reps within range, (2) Add load when top of range reached, (3) Add a set only if recovery supports it. Never add load AND volume simultaneously.

**Confidence:** Moderate-high.

---

### 8. Session Duration — Cortisol and Practical Limits

**Key literature:**
- The "cortisol spike after 60 minutes" is outdated. Originates from Kraemer et al. (1990s).
- **Schoenfeld (2013)**: Acute cortisol during training does NOT predict muscle loss when nutrition and recovery are adequate. Cortisol is problematic only when chronically elevated.
- No study identifies a specific minute threshold. The real issue is fatigue — later exercises suffer from accumulated fatigue.
- **Helms/Israetel**: 45-75 minutes recommended for quality of effort, not cortisol.

**Recommendation:** 45-65 minutes including warm-up. Cortisol is not a valid reason to cut sessions short. With 1-2 compounds and 2-4 accessories, 45-65 min is achievable.

**Confidence:** High.

---

### 9. Rest Periods

**Key literature:**
- **Schoenfeld et al. (2016)**: 3 min rest produced greater hypertrophy AND strength than 1 min, same volume.
- **Grgic et al. (2017)** meta-analysis: 2+ min superior for both strength and hypertrophy vs <1 min.
- **2024 Bayesian meta-analysis** (web-sourced above): Similar hypertrophy across 1-2 min, 2-3 min, 3+ min intervals. Strength modestly improved at 2+ min.
- **During a deficit**: Between-set recovery is slower. Argues for longer rest, not shorter.
- **Israetel**: "Turning lifting into cardio" by shortening rest is counterproductive — reduces load and volume quality.

**Recommendation:**
- Compounds: **2.5-4 minutes**. Err toward 3-4 min for heavy sets (RPE 8+).
- Accessories: **1.5-2.5 minutes**.
- Do not shorten rest to "burn more calories." Caloric cost is trivial. Performance per set matters.

**Note:** The 2024 Bayesian meta-analysis suggests hypertrophy may be similar at shorter rests. However, during a deficit the priority is preserving load quality, which favors longer rest. The 2024 data does not change the practical recommendation for this context.

**Confidence:** High.

---

### 10. Warm-Up Protocols

**Key literature:**
- **Fradkin et al. (2010)**: Warm-ups improve performance and may reduce injury risk.
- **McCrary et al. (2015)**: Specific warm-up sets more important than general warm-up.
- **Schoenfeld & Grgic (2020)**: 5-10 min general warm-up + ramping sets. No static stretching before lifting (reduces force 5-10%). Dynamic movement preferred.

**Warm-up set structure (Helms, Nuckols):**
- First compound: 2-4 ramping sets (bar x 10, 50% x 8, 70% x 5, 85% x 3, then work sets)
- Subsequent compounds: 1-2 ramping sets
- Accessories: 1 lighter set or none if muscles warm

**Recommendation:**
- 5 min general warm-up (rowing, bike, treadmill walk)
- 3-4 ramping sets for first compound
- 1-2 lighter sets for subsequent exercises
- No static stretching before lifting (post-session if desired)

**Confidence:** High.

### 11. Recent Developments (2023-2025) Shifting Conventional Wisdom

Confirmed shifts (integrating web-sourced data above):

1. **Body recomposition is more achievable than previously thought**, especially for lifters with higher BF%, training novelty, and high protein. This lifter is a strong candidate.

2. **Volume thresholds are individual**. The field has moved from universal prescriptions to recognizing MRV varies by individual, training history, deficit, sleep, stress. Start conservative, titrate by recovery.

3. **Training to failure is unnecessary** for hypertrophy during a deficit. Refalo et al. (2024) meta-regression and the 2024 RCT reinforce this.

4. **Longer rest periods for hypertrophy** is established, though the 2024 Bayesian analysis shows the hypertrophy advantage of long rest may be smaller than initially thought. During a deficit, longer rest still wins for load preservation.

5. **Protein timing window is wider** than previously thought. Total daily protein > peri-workout timing. Distribute across 3-5 meals at 0.3-0.5 g/kg per meal.

6. **Machine vs free weight equivalence** for hypertrophy is well-supported, giving more flexibility during a cut.

7. **Creatine during a cut** (2024 meta-analysis): meaningful lean mass preservation and fat loss benefit. 5 g/day throughout.

8. **Diet breaks** (MATADOR study): intermittent deficit (2 weeks on / 1-2 weeks maintenance) reduces metabolic adaptation. Align with deload weeks.

**What I could not verify (needs web search for late 2025-2026):**
- New meta-analyses published late 2025 or 2026
- New position papers from Schoenfeld, Helms, Israetel
- New RCTs on training during a deficit in intermediate lifters

### Summary: All Strength Training Recommendations

| Variable | Recommendation | Confidence |
|----------|---------------|------------|
| Volume | 10-12 sets/muscle/week, max ~14-16 | High |
| Frequency | 2x/week per muscle group | High |
| Intensity (RPE) | RPE 7-8.5 compounds, 7-8 accessories | High |
| Failure training | No systematic failure. 1-3 RIR default | High |
| Rep ranges | Mix: 3-6, 6-10, 10-15 across week (DUP) | Moderate-high |
| Exercise selection | 70-80% compound, 20-30% isolation/machine | High |
| Deload | Every 4-5 weeks, -40-50% volume, keep load | Moderate-high |
| Progressive overload | Realistic first 4-8 weeks, then maintenance focus | Moderate-high |
| Session duration | 45-65 minutes | High |
| Rest periods | 2.5-4 min compounds, 1.5-2.5 min accessories | High |
| Warm-up | 5 min general + 3-4 ramping sets first exercise | High |
| Creatine | 5 g/day monohydrate throughout | High |
| Diet breaks | 1 week at maintenance every 4-5 weeks (align with deload) | Moderate-high |
